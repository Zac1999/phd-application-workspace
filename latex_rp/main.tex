\section{Project Title}

\textbf{Design and Optimization of High-Reliability Driver Systems for GaN Power Integrated Circuits Based on DCFL Architecture}

\section{Project Summary}

This research adopts DCFL architecture to develop high-performance GaN power integrated driver circuits for industrial and automotive applications. Integrating multiple functional modules on a single chip optimizes driver performance in high-temperature and long-term working conditions. It solves key reliability problems of GaN such as high dV/dt immunity, thermal management and dynamic threshold voltage drift. Besides, an AI-assisted automated EKV model extraction method is established to optimize driver performance at different process nodes, which promotes the commercial application of GaN.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Research Context and Motivation}

The development of high-efficiency power electronic systems has become a key requirement in the global transition toward electrification and renewable energy. Gallium Nitride (GaN) High Electron Mobility Transistors (HEMTs) exert a transformative impact on next-generation power conversion. Compared with conventional silicon-based MOSFETs, they feature faster switching speeds, lower on-resistance, and higher breakdown voltages \cite{gao2024gan}. Benefiting from these properties, GaN has already achieved initial application prototypes in electric vehicle powertrains, renewable energy inverters, data center power supplies, industrial motor drives, and other fields, and is expected to become the mainstream solution in these areas in the foreseeable future.
Nevertheless, numerous integration challenges still prevent the full exploitation of the performance advantages of GaN power devices. In mainstream discrete device schemes, parasitic inductances restrict switching speed and efficiency, while the off-chip implementation of protection and sensing functions increases system complexity and cost. Monolithic integration of GaN power devices with control and protection circuits represents an important direction for advancing this technology \cite{liu2024monolithic}.

\subsection{Literature Review}

\subsubsection{GaN Power Device Technology}

GaN HEMTs leverage the AlGaN/GaN heterojunction to create a high-mobility two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) channel, enabling exceptional electron mobility and high current density \cite{ambacher1999two}. Two operational modes exist: Enhancement-mode (E-mode, normally-OFF) with positive threshold voltage, and Depletion-mode (D-mode, normally-ON) with negative threshold voltage. E-mode devices are preferred for power electronics due to their fail-safe operation, while D-mode devices serve as active loads in integrated circuits.

Recent advances have demonstrated E-mode GaN HEMTs with threshold voltages exceeding 1.5V, enabling robust logic-level compatibility. Peking University (2024) achieved 650V GaN-on-Silicon integrated circuits using virtual body isolation technology, demonstrating the feasibility of high-voltage monolithic integration \cite{peking2024virtual}. Liverpool University (2025) reported GaN DCFL circuits operating at 300°C with conversion efficiencies exceeding 90\%, highlighting the technology's potential for harsh environments \cite{liverpool2025high}.

\subsubsection{Direct-Coupled FET Logic (DCFL) for GaN Integration}

GaN high-electron-mobility transistors (HEMTs) feature a high-mobility two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) channel formed by the AlGaN/GaN heterojunction, and such 2DEG provides excellent electron mobility and high current density \cite{ambacher1999two}. Generally, GaN devices operate in two modes: enhancement-mode (E-mode, normally-OFF) with a positive threshold voltage, and depletion-mode (D-mode, normally-ON) with a negative threshold voltage. Enhancement-mode devices with fail-safe characteristics are widely used in power electronic systems, while depletion-mode devices are often employed as active loads in integrated circuits.

Recent studies have realized enhancement-mode GaN HEMTs with threshold voltages above 1.5 V and favorable logic-level compatibility. In 2024, Peking University developed 650 V GaN-on-silicon integrated circuits using virtual body isolation technology, verifying the feasibility of high-voltage monolithic integration \cite{peking2024virtual}. Meanwhile, in 2025, the University of Liverpool developed GaN DCFL circuits capable of operating at 300 °C with conversion efficiencies exceeding 90 percent, further demonstrating the promising potential of this technology in extreme environments such as industrial and aerospace applications \cite{liverpool2025high}.

\subsubsection{Protection and Sensing Circuits}

Modern GaN power integrated circuits rely heavily on protection mechanisms. Current sensing using the SenseFET architecture enables microsecond-level fault detection \cite{zhang2023sensefet}. The Three-Branch Level Shifter (TBLS) structure achieves high dV/dt immunity while maintaining low propagation delays \cite{zhang2022tbls}. Dynamic on-resistance degradation caused by charge trapping represents a critical reliability issue for GaN devices \cite{joh2014drain}.

\subsubsection{Research Gap}

Despite significant progress, critical gaps remain:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Limited Protection Functions:} Most existing studies focus primarily on individual circuit blocks, with little discussion on complete protection systems and interconnection mechanisms.
    \item \textbf{Insufficient High dV/dt Immunity:} Under actual switching conditions, issues concerning process isolation and signal crosstalk still persist in the integration of GaN power switches.
    \item \textbf{Lack of Automated Design Tools:} Current EKV model extraction relies on manual parameter fitting, limiting design efficiency and cross-process portability.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Process Portability:} EKV model extraction for different GaN process nodes requires repetitive manual effort, limiting design reuse.
    \item \textbf{Standardized Design Kits:} The absence of comprehensive PDKs limits accessibility of GaN IC design.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Objectives and Research Questions}

\subsection{Primary Objective}

To develop a comprehensive GaN power integrated circuit platform using DCFL architecture, integrating high-voltage power switches with intelligent protection, sensing, and drive functions, while establishing automated EKV model extraction methodologies using AI-assisted scripting for cross-process circuit optimization.

\subsection{Specific Research Questions}

\textbf{RQ1: Circuit Protection Architecture}\\
\textit{Question:} How can over-current, over-voltage, and over-temperature (OC/OV/OT) protection circuits be optimally integrated with GaN power HEMTs using DCFL technology to achieve rapid fault detection while minimizing silicon area overhead?\\
\textit{Hypothesis:} A protection system utilizing SenseFET-based current sensing, stacked high-voltage detection structures, and temperature-compensated reference circuits can achieve sub-microsecond response time with minimal area penalty through optimized circuit topology and layout techniques.

\textbf{RQ2: High Noise Immunity Level Shifting}\\
\textit{Question:} What circuit architectures can achieve high dV/dt immunity for high-side gate drive in 650V GaN half-bridge configurations while maintaining low propagation delays?\\
\textit{Hypothesis:} A modified Three-Branch Level Shifter (TBLS) with adaptive common-mode cancellation and integrated filtering can achieve enhanced dV/dt immunity with reduced propagation delay through capacitive isolation and differential common-mode rejection techniques.

\textbf{RQ3: Precision Current Sensing}\\
\textit{Question:} How can current mirror sensing circuits be optimized to achieve high accuracy across the full load range while compensating for device mismatch and parasitic inductance effects in integrated GaN current sensors?\\
\textit{Hypothesis:} The layout of Kelvin-connected SenseFETs can be optimized, and combined with current compensation algorithms, current sensing accuracy can be improved by calibrating process variations and temperature drift, or by adopting auxiliary circuit structures to eliminate current errors.

\textbf{RQ4: Thermal-Electrical Co-Design}\\
\textit{Question:} What is the impact of thermal-electrical coupling mechanisms in monolithic gallium nitride (GaN) power integrated circuits on practical applications? How can circuit optimization mitigate thermal-induced performance degradation and even device failure?\\
\textit{Hypothesis:} Compared with the conventional layout design of single GaN devices, adopting a distributed power device layout and forming an on-chip thermal-aware layout through integrated temperature sensing and adaptive gate drive circuits can effectively improve the thermal management performance of the entire chip and ensure its thermal stability.

\textbf{RQ5: Dynamic Reliability Enhancement}\\
\textit{Question:} How can circuit-level techniques compensate for the dynamic threshold voltage (Vth) shift and on-resistance (Rds(on)) degradation of GaN devices to maintain consistent switching performance over their lifetime?\\
\textit{Hypothesis:} Under typical operating conditions, adaptive gate drive circuits integrating real-time monitoring and dynamic gate voltage adjustment can provide effective compensation for dynamic performance degradation.

\textbf{RQ6: AI-Assisted Automated EKV Model Extraction}\\
\textit{Question:} How can artificial intelligence, based on EKV model parameter extraction, be utilized to rapidly evaluate chips with the same architecture across different gallium nitride (GaN) process nodes ($0.25 \mu m, 0.5 \mu m, 2 \mu m$), thereby accelerating circuit design iterations and enhancing cross-process portability?\\
\textit{Hypothesis:} Machine learning-based scripting tools can automatically extract EKV model parameters from measured device characteristics, thereby significantly reducing manual effort. Through automated parameter fitting and validation workflows, rapid optimization and porting of circuits with the same architecture across different GaN processes can be achieved.

\section{Theoretical Framework and Methods}

\subsection{Research Design}

This research adopts a systematic methodology that combines theoretical analysis, simulation-based design, and experimental validation through multiple tape-out iterations.

\textbf{Phase 1: Foundation and Circuit Design (Months 1--12)}
The work in the first phase includes conducting a literature review and completing the selected doctoral coursework, analyzing the foundry Process Design Kit (PDK) and device model characteristics, finalizing the circuit architecture design for Research Questions 1 to 5 (RQ1–RQ5), and carrying out the initial layout floor plan as well as single-device parasitic extraction.

\textbf{Phase 2: First Tape-out and Validation (Months 12--24)}
In the second phase, the first test chip tape-out (Tape-out \#1) is carried out to validate the individual circuit blocks, followed by post-silicon characterization and process-voltage-temperature (PVT) performance verification. Design iterations of the circuits are implemented based on the measured results, and the development of an AI-assisted tool for EKV parameter extraction is completed simultaneously to address Research Question 6 (RQ6).


\textbf{Phase 3: System Integration and Second Tape-out (Months 24--36)}
The work in the third phase includes conducting full-system integration design using validated circuit blocks and completing the system-level design of protection functions, followed by the second test chip tape-out (Tape-out \#2) for integrated system validation, realizing cross-process design porting with the aid of automated EKV parameter extraction tools, and carrying out the third test chip tape-out (Tape-out \#3) to verify performance under different processes.


\textbf{Phase 4: Final Optimization and Completion (Months 36--48)}
The work in the fourth phase involves conducting final design optimization based on comprehensive test results, performing the fourth test chip tape-out (Tape-out \#4) to complete the verification of the final system as well as reliability and yield verification, and preparing for dissertation writing and defense.

\subsection{Methodology by Research Question}

\subsubsection{RQ1--RQ5: Circuit Design and Validation Methodology}

\textbf{Stage 1: Specification and Architecture (Months 1--6)}
The work in the first phase includes defining performance specifications according to industrial and aerospace applications, conducting architectural exploration based on behavioral modeling to complete topology selection and initial feasibility analysis.


\textbf{Stage 2: Schematic Design and Simulation (Months 6--12)}
The work in this phase includes performing transistor-level design using Cadence Virtuoso, conducting corner analysis and Monte Carlo analysis to ensure the robustness of the architecture and circuits, and taking electromigration (EM) and IR drop into account in pre-simulation to complete the corresponding design verification.


\textbf{Stage 3: Layout and Pre-Tape-out Verification (Months 10--14)}
The work in this phase includes performing custom layout design with a focus on device matching and the matching between parasitic parameters and the circuit architecture, and conducting post-layout simulation with extracted parasitics after completing Design Rule Checking (DRC) and Layout Versus Schematic (LVS) verification.

\textbf{Stage 4: Post-Silicon Validation (Months 14--20, 26--32, 38--44)}
The work in this phase includes performing wafer-level probing tests, DC characterization, high-frequency and switching performance tests, and temperature and reliability tests in accordance with the designed test plan, and finally conducting correlation analysis between simulation and measurement results based on the test outcomes.


\subsubsection{RQ6: AI-Assisted EKV Model Extraction Methodology}

\textbf{Step 1: Measurement Database Generation}
The work in first step includes extracting device measurement data and EKV parameters using conventional methods from multiple process nodes including $0.25\mu m, 0.5\mu m and 2\mu m$, covering geometry variations and operating conditions ranging from -40°C to 200°C, with a target dataset size of more than 500 device instances for each process node.


\textbf{Step 2: AI-Assisted Scripting Tool Development}
The second step includes developing Python-based automation scripts integrated with ensemble regression algorithms, conducting feature engineering covering device geometry parameters ($L_g$, $W_g$), bias conditions ($V_{gs}$, $V_{ds}$), temperature, and process node identifiers, setting the target accuracy metrics for all EKV parameters ($I_S$, $V_{T0}$, $\kappa$, $\eta$) as a coefficient of determination ($R^2$) greater than 0.95 and a root mean square error (RMSE) less than 5\%, and developing an interface that supports batch processing and cross-node comparison to facilitate process comparison.


\textbf{Step 3: Validation and Integration}
In the third step, a hold-out test set accounting for 20\% of the total data will be adopted to validate the accuracy of the automated extraction results against the manually extracted parameters; correlation analysis will be performed between the predicted and measured device characteristics; the automated extraction function will be integrated into the circuit design flow within the Cadence Virtuoso environment; and the cross-process portability of the proposed scheme will be demonstrated through design case studies (e.g., porting a level shifter across three different process nodes).


\subsection{Equipment and Resources}
The simulation tools utilized in this research include Cadence Virtuoso, Keysight ADS, and COMSOL Multiphysics. For device fabrication, this study has access to the in-house 2-$\mu$m GaN process platform at Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool University (XJTLU), as well as external foundry partnerships supporting 0.25-$\mu$m and 0.5-$\mu$m processes. Device characterization is performed using semiconductor parameter analyzers, high-voltage pulse generators, thermal imaging equipment, and probe stations.
